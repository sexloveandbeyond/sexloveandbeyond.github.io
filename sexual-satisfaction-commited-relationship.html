Sexual satisfaction in committed relationships

December 5th, 2018

This article seeks to explain how sexual satisfaction is created in committed relationships and how sexual satisfaction or dissatisfaction affects relationship happiness and durability. Defining the terms is an important part of this endeavor; however, the ability to do so is somewhat limited. For example, the word satisfaction can be defined in various ways and satisfaction may mean different things to different people. Nonetheless, because of a presumption that everyone knows what it means to say that someone is satisfied or dissatisfied, much of the literature on sexual satisfaction and relationship satisfaction never really defines the word. In most instances, the measure of satisfaction is merely a question: How satisfied are you with your sex life together? Or, in reference to another assumed understanding, researchers might ask, How happy would you say you are with your relationship right now? (McNulty & Fisher, 2008). The idea here is that a subjective assessment using open-ended questions is a better indicator of satisfaction than a set of closed-ended questions, such as those that might be used in a clinical test for depression (e.g., Do you wake up in the morning ready and willing to face the day?; Do you think your life is worse, average, or better than that of the people you know?). These latter kinds of assessments are more or less unknown in the sexual satisfaction literature. Indeed, large-scale studies on general happiness (e.g., Yang, 2008) also follow the subjective assessment model. Following these traditions—and because we are reviewing previous research rather than doing our own—we will look at sexual and relationship satisfaction as subjective assessments. In a few cases we will consider negative subjective assessments (e.g., depression or sadness), but for the most part we are interested in the correlations between sexual behavior, other relationship behavior, and partners’ feelings about their sexual life and relationship as a couple.

Without referring to the nonsexual satisfaction and happiness literatures, people intuitively know what researchers have discovered: Satisfaction and happiness are correlated with mental and physical health. Furthermore, research (Blumstein & Schwartz, 1983; HendersonKing & Veroff, 1994; Kurdek, 1991; Lawrence & Byers, 1995) has shown a general correlation between sexual satisfaction and relationship satisfaction in both sameand opposite-sex couples. However, just as researchers are not exactly sure which aspects of daily life produce personal happiness, they remain a bit hazy—though not clueless—on exactly what contributes to people’s sexual satisfaction. Research has indicated, for example, that sexual dissatisfaction is correlated with extramarital sexuality (Bringle & Buunk, 1991) and that extramarital sexuality places both same-sex and opposite-sex couples at higher risk for dissolution (Blumstein & Schwartz). Mutual monogamy is a cultural mandate in the United States, Canada, most of Europe, and many other parts of the world. Mandated monogamy for women is almost universal. Research conducted in the United States (Laumann, Gagnon, Michael, & Michaels, 1994) has indicated that more than 85% of both men and women believe that any committed relationship—but especially marriage—requires monogamy. The findings that nonmonogamy is stigmatized and creates risk of crisis in relationships, plus the fact that the chances for nonmonogamy are higher when couples are dissatisfied with their sexual relationship, are powerful reasons to pursue more information about what produces sexual satisfaction within relationships. Furthermore, we believe that sexuality has become a much more important factor in people’s self-assessment of personal happiness. Although not applicable to all parts of the world, in advanced capitalist societies such as the United States, Japan, Australia, and Europe, sex—as marketing people so often say—sells. The average citizen is barraged with ads that link products with sexual success and even with sexual adequacy. This message can be subtle—or not: P. Diddy, a popular performer and musician, advertised his new line of clothes on the May 18, 2008, edition of CBS Morning News as fashion Viagra (Spoil The Ending, 2008). The marketing of most products as sexual aids (e.g., a 2008 Cadillac commercial tag line: When you turn your car on, does it return the favor?) means that even morally conservative people cannot avoid media messaging that links sexuality with the so-called good life. Leonore Tiefer’s (2001) analysis of this situation went even deeper: She wrote that sexuality is now defined as the core of people’s identity and has been made synonymous with competent enactment of masculinity and femininity. In other words, given the emphasis on sexuality as part of social success, cultural capital, selfworth, and gender normality, it is hard to imagine that sexual satisfaction could be anything but important to personal and relationship happiness. In fact, to go a bit further, we believe that good sex—and, perhaps, great sex—has become one of adult men’s and women’s expectations in life. This expectation becomes especially poignant in long-term committed relationships. Finally, we also believe that the surfeit of sexual information provided via movies, books, and Internet sites, as well as the almost universal experience of premarital sexual experience by the age of 21 in the United States and other industrialized countries, (Laumann et al., 1994), has increased individual expectations about sexuality and has provided a comparative framework for assessing one’s partner’s abilities as a lover. At the same time that sexual satisfaction is being touted as a right, it is being glamorized in various media outlets. The result: Individuals’ own basis of comparison has likely given way to even higher standards for the quality of sexuality in their relationships. These expectations may be dashed if certain aspects of sex, such as frequency of intercourse or amount of affectionate behavior, are perceived as deficient. By examining existing research, we seek to determine what troubles or supports sexual satisfaction and how those factors affect couples’ short- and long-term happiness, as well as their continuing commitment to each other. To that end, this article takes a closer look at the elements of personal expectations, sexual performance, identity, gender, desire, and values that affect sexual satisfaction for individuals and couples. We also review literature indicating what outside forces might make meeting sexual expectations harder or easier, as well as research examining other aspects of couples’ lives that affect sexual satisfaction.

Dr. Rhonda Lewis

Professor of Psychology, researching the fields of sex, love and relationships
